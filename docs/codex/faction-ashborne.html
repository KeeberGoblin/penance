The Ashborne Engines

The Iron Pilgrims - Bonus Secret Faction

"They say we're monsters for hunting the weak. But the world ended because humanity stood still. We survive because we move. We eat or we starve. The tracks don't stop rolling, and neither do we." -Captain Vrill Stoke, Commandant of the Devourer-Class city "Ashfang"
✿

Faction Overview

Aspect Details
Philosophy "Municipal Darwinism" - The strong consume the weak. Cities hunt cities. Movement is survival.
Population ~800,000 spread across 47 mobile cities (12 Predator-Class, 23 Hunter-Class, 12 Scavenger-Class)
Territory The Ashen Barrens (former industrial heartlands of Eurasia) - nomadic, no fixed settlements
Technology Steam-powered everything. Massive mobile cities on tracks. Coal/wood-fired Caskets. NO Soulstones.
Power Source High-pressure steam, coal combustion, geothermal vents, salvaged pre-Sundering engines
Governance Each city ruled by a Captain-Commandant. Intercity hierarchy based on tonnage and prey consumed.
Religion The Doctrine of Consumption - "To eat is holy. To stop is death. The tracks are eternal."
Primary Threat Fuel starvation, mechanical breakdown, being hunted by larger cities, Void corruption of boilers

Why This Faction is Secret

The Ashborne Engines operate in the Ashen Barrens, far from the territories controlled by the ten major factions. Most survivors believe they're myths...ghost cities glimpsed on the horizon, tracks carved through wastelands, entire settlements vanishing overnight. Only traders who've ventured into the Barrens return with tales of "cities that walk" and "iron beasts that eat villages whole."

Faction Status: Tier 3 (Minor Power) - isolated, self-sufficient, technologically divergent. They don't participate in Soulstone economy or factional politics. They simply consume and move.

The Philosophy of Municipal Darwinism

Core Tenets

  1. "Stasis is Death" - Cities that don't move become targets. Populations that settle are prey.
  2. "Consumption is Survival" - Resources are finite. The strong take from the weak. This is natural law.
  3. "The Tracks are Eternal" - As long as your city moves, your people survive. Stop moving, and you die.
  4. "Soulstones are Slavery" - They reject binding souls as power sources. "We burn coal, not people."
  5. "Size Determines Right" - Larger cities have first claim on prey. Smaller cities flee or are consumed.
"The Sundering taught us one truth: civilization that roots itself dies when the ground shakes. So we built cities that move. Cities that hunt. Cities that survive by devouring the foolish who stand still." -The Codex of Consumption, foundational text

Anatomy of a Predator-Class City

The Tracks

Massive segmented treads, 40+ feet wide, driven by colossal steam pistons. Cities move at 5-15 mph when hunting, 2-5 mph when cruising. Tracks carve permanent scars across landscape.

The Gut Furnaces

Heart of the city. 20-50 massive boilers burning coal, wood, scrap metal, organic waste. Anything combustible. Temperature: 1,200-1,800°F. Crew: 200+ stokers per shift.

The Jaws

Front-mounted cutting/crushing machinery. Tears down buildings, processes prey cities into manageable chunks. Hydraulic mandibles can lift 500+ tons.

The Spine

Central superstructure housing factories, living quarters, storage. Vertical city rising 100-300 feet. Lower decks = furnaces and engine rooms. Upper decks = elite quarters and command bridge.

The Arsenal

Steam-powered Caskets (20-50 units per Predator), harpoon cannons, coal-gas projectors, boarding bridges. Grimsteam cities are mobile fortresses.

The Chimneys

12-30 smokestacks venting exhaust. Visible for 20+ miles. A city's "roar" can be heard before it's seen. Black smoke = coal. Gray = wood. Red = they're burning corpses.

"First you hear the smoke. Then you see the smoke. Then you hear the tracks. By the time you see the city itself, it's too late to run." -Survivor testimony, Village of Emberfall (consumed Year 412)

Steam-Powered Caskets: The Ashforged

Technology Philosophy

Ashborne engineers reject Soulstones as "necromantic parasitism." Instead, they power Caskets with high-pressure steam generated from coal-fired boilers mounted on the chassis. These "Ashforged" units are heavier, louder, and require constant refueling...but they're immune to Taint corruption and don't require enslaved souls.

Ashforged Casket Design

Component Function Grimsteam Version
Power Source Energy generation Back-mounted coal boiler (300-500 lbs of coal per 8-hour operation). Steam pressure: 800-1,200 PSI.
Actuators Limb movement Hydraulic pistons driven by steam pressure. Slower than Soulstone hydraulics but incredibly powerful (can lift 3x standard Casket capacity).
Pilot Interface Control system Mechanical levers, pressure gauges, valve wheels. No neural threads - purely physical control. Pilots must be exceptionally strong.
Armor Protection Riveted iron plates 2-4 inches thick. Heavy but durable. Resistant to Void corruption (no souls to taint).
Weaponry Combat Steam-powered pile drivers, coal-gas flamethrowers, harpoon launchers, superheated exhaust vents as area denial.
Operational Time Mission duration 6-8 hours before refueling required. Pilots must carry coal sacks or retreat to supply depot.

Ashforged Advantages

  • No Taint Accumulation: Pilots never suffer Void corruption (no soul link = no corruption vector)
  • Incredible Strength: Steam pressure generates 30-50% more lifting/crushing force than standard hydraulics
  • Intimidation Factor: The roar of venting steam, billowing smoke, glowing furnace = psychological warfare
  • Fuel Independence: Any combustible material works (wood, coal, corpses, scrap). No need for rare Soulstones.
  • Simplicity: Mechanical systems can be field-repaired by competent engineers. No soul-binding specialists needed.

Ashforged Disadvantages

  • Fuel Dependency: Constant refueling required. Pilots who run out of coal are stranded.
  • Weight: +40% heavier than Soulstone Caskets (boiler + coal + water = massive bulk)
  • Slow Startup: Boiler takes 15-30 minutes to reach operating pressure from cold start
  • Visible Exhaust: Smoke trail reveals position for miles. Stealth impossible.
  • Heat Signature: Glowing boiler makes pilots vulnerable to thermal detection
  • Physical Strain: Operating mechanical levers for hours = extreme pilot fatigue
"A Soulstone Casket is a puppet with strings of pain. An Ashforged is a beast you wrestle into submission. Every lever pull, every valve turn...you EARN the machine's obedience. It doesn't love you. It doesn't hate you. It simply is. And that's freedom." -Pilot-Engineer Karn, instructor at Ashfang Academy

Daily recitation of Municipal Darwinism tenets. "To stop is death. To move is life. The strong consume. The weak are consumed."

Trophy Walls

Cities display nameplates/artifacts from consumed prey. Ashfang has 147 plaques. "We remember what we ate."

Lore Integration: How They Fit in Year 437

Origins (Year 12-50)

When the Sundering shattered civilization, the survivors who inhabited the pre-Sundering industrial heartlands (former Germany, Poland, Czech regions) faced a choice: flee to safer lands or adapt to the Ashen Barrens. One faction, led by railroad engineer Maximilian Grue, proposed a radical solution: make the cities mobile.

Using salvaged pre-Sundering locomotives, excavators, and factory equipment, Grue's followers built the first Hunter-Class city, "The Pioneer," in Year 27. It was crude, slow, and consumed more fuel than it could scavenge...but it moved. And movement meant survival.

Over the next century, the Ashborne philosophy spread. Static settlements in the Barrens were systematically consumed by mobile cities. By Year 150, the region had transformed into a predatory ecosystem where only moving cities survived.

Current Status (Year 437)

  • Population: ~800,000 spread across 47 mobile cities
  • Territory: The Ashen Barrens (a 2,000-mile-wide wasteland of industrial ruins, coal seams, and scarred earth)
  • Economy: Self-sufficient through consumption. Rarely trade with outsiders (viewed as "static prey").
  • Threat Level: Regional menace. Don't expand beyond Barrens but will consume any settlement foolish enough to enter their territory.
  • Secrecy: Most factions dismiss them as legend. Only Nomad Collective and Exchange traders confirm their existence.

Why Haven't They Been Destroyed?

  1. Geographic Isolation: The Barrens are hostile to outsiders (ash storms, Void rifts, no resources worth conquering).
  2. Mobility: You can't siege a city that moves 15 mph. By the time an army arrives, the city has relocated.
  3. No Strategic Value: They don't control Soulstone deposits, fertile land, or trade routes. Major factions ignore them.
  4. Mutual Deterrence: Ashborne cities avoid major faction territories. Factions avoid the Barrens. Uneasy equilibrium.
"Let them have their wastelands. Let them eat each other. As long as they stay in the Barrens, they're someone else's problem." -Forge-Guild Council deliberation, Year 341

Gameplay Mechanics (For Players)

Playing as Ashborne Characters

Faction Traits

  • Taint Immunity: Ashborne pilots never accumulate Taint (no Soulstone link). Immune to Void corruption mechanics.
  • Fuel Dependency: Must track Coal supply (1 Coal = 1 hour of Ashforged operation). Running out of Coal mid-mission = stranded.
  • Brute Strength: Ashforged Caskets gain +2 to all Strength-based actions but suffer -1 to Agility/Speed actions.
  • Outsider Status: Other factions view Ashborne as barbaric scavengers. -2 to social interactions with non-Nomad factions.
  • Mechanical Expertise: +2 to repairing/maintaining Caskets (purely mechanical systems, no soul-binding knowledge needed).

Campaign Hooks: Encountering the Ashborne

Scenario 1: The Hunted Village

Players are hired to defend a border settlement reporting "a city on the horizon that moves." Refugees flee, telling impossible stories of buildings on treads. The village elder begs: "Stop it before it reaches us." Players must choose: evacuate the village, fortify and fight, or negotiate with the approaching Predator-Class city (which may or may not honor any deal).

Scenario 2: Ashborne Defector

A Hunter-Pilot named Vex has fled their mobile city, stealing an Ashforged Casket. They seek asylum with players' faction, offering steam technology secrets in exchange for protection. But their former Captain-Commandant sends pursuit teams, demanding Vex's return. Players must choose: shelter a deserter (making an enemy of an entire mobile city) or surrender them (and lose access to steam Casket tech).

Scenario 3: The Iron Pilgrimage

A Scavenger-Class city, desperate for fuel, ventures beyond the Barrens into faction territory. They offer trade: coal, salvaged pre-Sundering tech, Grimforged blueprints in exchange for food and safe passage. But their presence violates territorial agreements. Players must negotiate between the mobile city and their faction's leaders...knowing that if talks fail, the city will consume the nearest settlement to refuel before fleeing.

Scenario 4: The Long Hunt

A young Ashborne pilot on their proving trial (The Long Hunt) challenges players to single combat. They don't want to kill...they want to test themselves against "outsiders who fight with soul-chains." Victory earns their respect (and possible alliance). Defeat means the pilot returns home with proof that Soulstone Caskets are superior (boosting Ashborne interest in raiding faction territories for "better prey").

Moral Complexity: Are They Villains?

The Ashborne Dilemma

On the surface, the Ashborne Engines are predatory monsters who hunt the weak. But consider:

  • They don't use Soulstones: Unlike other factions, they refuse to enslave souls. "Coal may be dirty, but it's honest."
  • They offer assimilation: Prey populations can join cities as Gut Crews (brutal work, but survival). Not all consumed settlements are killed.
  • They're survivors: The Ashen Barrens have no resources. Mobility and consumption are the only survival strategies that work.
  • They're consistent: The Ashborne never lie about their nature. "We hunt. We consume. We survive. This is the law."

Are they more honest than the Church (who enslave souls while preaching redemption)? More pragmatic than the Ossuarium (who hoard the dead)? Or are they simply predators with better PR?

That's for players to decide.

"The Church binds souls and calls it salvation. The Ossuarium raises corpses and calls it efficiency. The Dwarves hoard resources and call it progress. We hunt settlements and call it survival. Who's the real monster? The one who admits what they are, or the one who lies?" -Captain Vrill Stoke, interrogated by Church Inquisition (Year 429)
