Casket Technology: Walking Coffins of the New Age

"We wear the dead on our backs. We fight with their stolen strength. And we call it survival." —Pilot Epitaph, Church of Absolution

Caskets are humanity's most powerful—and most horrifying—technology. These soul-powered war machines are the only military equipment capable of fighting Abominations, controlling territory, and projecting power in the post-Sundering world. They are also mobile prisons for the dead, binding souls to eternal service within mechanical bodies.

The Three Pillars of Casket Technology

1. Pre-Sundering Origins

Caskets began as industrial exoskeletons—heavy-duty powered armor for construction, mining, and disaster response. Companies like Titan Industries and Prometheus Corp built them for civilian use, never military applications. They survived the Theslar EMP because their fusion cells were radiation-shielded (industrial safety requirement) and their hydraulics were mechanical, not electronic.

The Irony: The most primitive modern technology became the most advanced post-Sundering technology. Construction equipment became war machines.

2. Soul-Binding Discovery

On Year 0, Day 6, Dr. Marcus Thresher (later Bonelord Thresh) made the breakthrough: souls could be bound to mechanical systems as power sources. What began as desperate improvisation—connecting his dead wife's nervous system to an exoskeleton—became the foundation of modern civilization. Read the full discovery story here.

3. The Deck System

Caskets are controlled through memory cards—crystallized fragments of a soul's skills and experiences. The deck represents the pilot's vitality. Draw too many cards (burn too many memories), and the soul exhausts itself. Run out of cards entirely, and the soul burns out—permanent death with no afterlife.

How Caskets Work

Anatomy of a Casket

Component Function
Soul Core Resonance chamber containing bound soul (center of chassis, most protected). Damage = soul released = instant shutdown
Chassis Reinforced steel/titanium frame (800-2,400 lbs depending on class: Scout, Assault, Heavy, Support)
Limbs Hydraulic-powered arms/legs (modular, replaceable, faction-customized)
Head Optics, audio sensors, targeting systems (no brain—soul core controls everything)
Deck System Cards represent soul's memories/skills. Deck size = soul's vitality. 0 cards = soul burns out = death
Armor Plating Faction-specific design (Church: blessed steel, Dwarves: runic plating, Elves: living wood, etc.)

The Soul Experience

What does it feel like to be a bound soul piloting a Casket? According to rare Ossuarium interviews:

  • Vision: See through optical sensors (monochrome, thermal, or night vision)
  • Hearing: Hear through audio pickups (can't speak verbally, radio transmission only)
  • Touch: Feel pressure/damage via hydraulic feedback (phantom pain)
  • Taste/Smell: None (sensory deprivation is the worst part)
  • Emotion: Ranges from acceptance to rage to despair
  • Time Perception: Distorted (some souls experience centuries as minutes)
  • Degradation: Soul's memory/personality erodes over 20-50 years
"The horror isn't the violence. It's the boredom. Between battles, you sit in darkness. No sleep. No dreams. No death. Just waiting for the next time someone needs you to kill." —Anonymous Soul Interview, Ossuarium Archives

The Moral Debate

Arguments For Soul-Binding

  • Survival Necessity: Without Caskets, humanity cannot fight Abominations or defend territory
  • Voluntary Contracts: Many souls agree to binding in exchange for compensation to their families
  • Honored Service: Pilots are celebrated as heroes, their sacrifice recognized
  • Alternative to True Death: Souls continue existing rather than facing oblivion

Arguments Against Soul-Binding

  • Slavery: Souls are trapped, unable to rest or move on to afterlife
  • Exploitation: The desperate sign contracts they don't understand
  • Degradation: Souls lose memories, personality, humanity over time
  • Violation of Death: Weaponizing the afterlife is fundamentally wrong
"Every Casket pilot is a prisoner. Some cells are gilded. Some are honored. But they're all prisons. And we built them because we were too desperate to ask if we should." —Rootwarden Kess
